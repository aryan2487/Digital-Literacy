Unified Payments Interface (UPI) has revolutionized digital transactions in India, but its convenience is often exploited by cybercriminals through social engineering. This case study explores a common "Request Money" scam targeting unsuspecting secondary-market sellers.

The crime typically begins on online classified platforms (like OLX or Facebook Marketplace). A fraudster poses as a highly motivated buyer for an item listed by the victim. To build trust, the criminal often claims to be an army officer or a busy professional who cannot meet in person but wants to "secure" the item immediately by sending an advance payment.

Initial Contact: The fraudster contacts the seller and agrees to the full asking price without negotiation to prevent the victim from becoming suspicious.
The "Payment" Trap: Instead of sending money, the fraudster sends a QR code via WhatsApp, claiming the victim needs to "scan it to receive the payment".
The Theft: When the victim scans the code and enters their UPI PIN, they are actually authorizing a debit from their own account rather than a credit. The fraudster immediately withdraws the maximum permissible limit before the victim realizes the balance has dropped.

The primary consequence is the immediate loss of funds, which are often moved through multiple "mule" accounts, making recovery difficult for local authorities. Beyond financial loss, victims often experience significant psychological distress and a loss of trust in digital banking systems.

While anyone can be a target, college students and elderly individuals—who may be familiar with technology but unfamiliar with the specific "Receive vs. Send" mechanics of UPI—are most vulnerable.
